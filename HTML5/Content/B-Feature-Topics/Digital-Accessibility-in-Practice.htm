Digital Accessibility in Practice

Legal compliance sets a floor, but it should not be the ceiling. The stronger reason to care about accessibility is a human one: real people are affected by every content decision you make.

Great design starts by thinking about how to make products work for everyone (Horton and Quesenbery 1). The book introduces a set of personas to make the principles of accessibility concrete. Each persona also connects to one of four core accessibility principles, known by the acronym POUR: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust. These principles come from WCAG and describe the qualities that accessible content must have. Four of Horton and Quesenbery’s personas illustrate how different users experience the web, and what is at stake for real people without it.

Steven

Steven is a 38-year-old graphic artist who is deaf and communicates primarily in American Sign Language (ASL). ASL is a complete, independent language with its own grammar and structure; it is not a signed version of English. Because Steven grew up using ASL rather than written English, dense blocks of text can be difficult for him to read, and he prefers content with clear visual organization. He also relies on captions to access any audio or video content online. Captions are text versions of the spoken words and sounds in a video, displayed on screen in sync with the audio. When a product tutorial or training video is published without captions, Steven has no way to access that information (Horton and Quesenbery 10-11). Steven illustrates the Perceivable principle from a different angle, showing that audio content needs a text equivalent, or it is simply unavailable to deaf users.

Trevor

Trevor is an 18-year-old high school student with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), a neurological condition that affects how a person communicates, processes sensory information, and navigates social situations. Trevor reads slowly and very literally. Figurative language, idioms, and metaphors can genuinely confuse him. Moving images and cluttered page layouts are distracting and make it hard for him to focus on the text. He navigates websites best when they are consistent, predictable, and free of unnecessary visual noise (Horton and Quesenbery 2-3). Trevor’s experience reflects the Understandable principle, that content should use plain language, predictable structure, and minimal distractions so that it can be understood by users with a range of cognitive differences.

Lea

Lea is a 35-year-old writer who lives with fibromyalgia, a condition that causes chronic, widespread pain and fatigue. On difficult days, she must carefully manage her energy to get through her work. She uses voice recognition software called Dragon NaturallySpeaking, which lets her dictate text and control her computer entirely by voice rather than typing. She also uses a specialized keyboard designed to reduce strain on her wrists and hands (Horton and Quesenbery 8-9). Lea’s situation reflects the Operable principle, which surrounds users who cannot rely on a standard keyboard or mouse and need digital content that can be navigated in other ways, including by voice command or keyboard shortcuts alone.

Accessible Content Is Better Content for Everyone

A third reason to prioritize accessibility is that it improves content for a much wider audience than just users with disabilities. This idea is sometimes described using the example of curb cuts, which were designed specifically to help wheelchair users, but they also make things easier for people pushing strollers, workers pulling carts, cyclists stepping off their bikes, and anyone carrying something heavy. Digital content works similarly, the captions on a video help a viewer in a noisy environment, not just a deaf viewer. A clear heading structure lets a busy reader skim to the section they need, not just a screen reader user. Designing for constrained or underserved users tends to improve the experience for everyone.
